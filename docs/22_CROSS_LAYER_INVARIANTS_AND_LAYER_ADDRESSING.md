# 22 — Cross-Layer Invariants and Layer Addressing

Author: Mark Charles McLaughlin  
Framework: McLaughlin–Kairos Unified Field Theory  
Status: Silver Update public structural addendum  
Role: prevent fragmentation by distinguishing one invariant from its different expressions across S–I–P–O.

---

## 1. Core rule

A single invariant can appear differently at different layers without becoming several unrelated mechanisms.

> One invariant. Many layer addresses. No fragmentation.

This rule is required because unified models often fail in one of two ways:

- they flatten every layer into the same thing;
- or they rename one repeated structure as several disconnected theories.

MKUFT uses layer addressing to avoid both errors.

---

## 2. Formal statement

Let `K` be a candidate invariant structure.

Let `L ∈ {S, I, P, O}` identify the active layer.

Let `θ_L` represent the constraints, observables, units, noise sources, and admissible transitions specific to that layer.

The layer expression is:

```text
K_L = A_L(K ; θ_L)
```

where `A_L` is an address map, not a new ontology.

`K_L` should preserve the defining relation of `K` while changing its measurable form according to the layer.

A valid cross-layer invariant therefore requires:

1. a stable core relation;
2. explicit layer-specific variables;
3. a lawful address map;
4. no silent change of meaning;
5. layer-appropriate evidence;
6. independent falsifiers at each address.

---

## 3. S–I–P–O discipline

### S — Substrate

The substrate is the structured possibility or source domain.

It is not automatically a physical grid, cognitive image, symbolic diagram, or informational database.

A substrate claim must remain distinct from the post-boundary geometry used to represent admissible states and transitions.

### I — Information

The information layer carries pattern, relation, address, role, state, rule, routing, permission, and executable structure.

An invariant expressed here should be stated in terms of informational constraint and transition, not imported physical force language unless a physical coupling is separately established.

### P — Physical

The physical layer carries matter, energy, field behaviour, timing, instruments, bodies, environments, and measurable effects.

A physical address must recover ordinary physics in ordinary conditions and must specify units, controls, expected effect size, and reduction rules.

### O — Observer

The observer layer carries attention, interpretation, measurement context, memory, salience, coherence, and bounded selection among admissible paths.

An observer address does not grant unlimited causal power and must not replace a missing physical mechanism.

---

## 4. Addressing test

When the same term appears in more than one layer, ask:

```text
What is invariant?
What changes with the address?
What is measured here?
What is only analogous?
What coupling connects the addresses?
What would falsify this address without destroying the others?
```

If those questions cannot be answered, the model may be using one word for several unrelated things.

If the answers are identical at every layer, the model may be flattening real differences.

## 5. LUCY as the primary worked example

LUCY means Local Unified Coherence Yield.

Core invariant:

> LUCY is the local threshold at which coherence becomes strong enough to change boundary behaviour.

The invariant is singular. Its addresses are not separate LUCYs.

Useful notation:

- `LUCY(I)` — informational, routing, or executable-structure coherence;
- `LUCY(O)` — observer-state coherence, including OCQS gating;
- `LUCY(P)` — physical or material coherence.

The layer-addressed coherence yield is:

```text
C_L(x,t) = χ_L ||∇τ_L(x,t)||² / (N_L(x,t) + ε)
```

where:

- `τ_L` = the relevant post-boundary tension or compression potential at layer `L`;
- `∇τ_L` = the local gradient across the active boundary;
- `χ_L` = layer-specific coupling sensitivity;
- `N_L` = layer-specific noise, contradiction, disorder, decoherence, or turbulence;
- `ε` = a small stabiliser.

Threshold condition:

```text
C_L(x,t) ≥ C_*
```

Below threshold, ordinary layer behaviour dominates.

At threshold, a stable boundary condition may form or change.

The shared invariant is the coherence-to-boundary transition. The variables and evidence differ by address.

## 6. LUCY(I)

At the information layer:

- `τ_I` = tension between possible interpretations, routes, states, or executions;
- `∇τ_I` = the gradient created when one route becomes structurally favoured;
- `N_I` = ambiguity, contradiction, drift, noise, mis-addressing, or unresolved state conflict.

LUCY(I) is approached when scattered information locks into a stable executable pattern and drift becomes visible by contrast.

Plain English:

> The pattern stops being loose. It acquires a boundary and a route.

Possible tests include:

- improved execution consistency;
- lower interpretation variance;
- stronger error detection;
- reduced route ambiguity;
- stable reconstruction from partial information.

## 7. LUCY(O)

At the observer layer:

- `τ_O` = tension among competing attentional, interpretive, emotional, and memory states;
- `∇τ_O` = the gradient toward a stable observer configuration;
- `N_O` = distraction, contradiction, fear contamination, expectation bias, fatigue, and internal noise.

LUCY(O) is approached when the observer becomes coherent enough that attention, interpretation, and action stop fighting one another.

OCQS can be treated as one observer-state gating condition relevant to this address.

Possible tests include:

- lower internal contradiction;
- improved signal discrimination;
- more stable judgement under pressure;
- reduced impulsive frame-switching;
- better calibration between confidence and accuracy.

Observer coherence remains bounded. It does not permit arbitrary physical outcomes.

## 8. LUCY(P)

At the physical layer:

- `τ_P` = physical boundary tension or coherence potential;
- `∇τ_P` = the physical gradient across the coherent boundary;
- `N_P` = thermal disorder, decoherence, vibration, electromagnetic noise, material impurity, or field turbulence.

Candidate test systems include:

- superconductors;
- superfluids;
- Bose–Einstein-condensate-like systems;
- coherent mass-current systems;
- geometry-controlled boundary media.

LUCY(P) is weakened if coherent physical systems show no boundary-linked deviation beyond known physics, no scaling with reduced noise, and no reproducible geometry dependence.

It is strengthened only by controlled, pre-registered, independently reproduced effects.

## 9. Group coherence is not a new layer

A group is a coupled system across existing layers.

A group event may include:

- O-layer individual states;
- P-layer bodies, environment, timing, and signal channels;
- I-layer shared symbols, instructions, and coordination.

The S-layer remains the underlying possibility domain. Group coherence does not create a separate direct group-substrate mechanism by assertion.

The existing group form remains:

```text
κ_group = Φ(κ_1, ..., κ_N, C_corr)
```

A simple approximation is:

```text
κ_group = A_align × (1/N) Σ κ_i
```

with environmental damping:

```text
κ_eff,group = κ_group × η(F)
```

This is a coupled address, not a fifth layer.

## 10. Post-boundary lattice discipline

Do not describe the undifferentiated substrate as a literal lattice, grid, mesh, or geometry unless independent evidence requires it.

Correct public wording:

> Lattice-like geometry appears post-boundary as a representation of admissible states, adjacency relations, routing paths, coherence envelopes, and traversal costs.

A physical or informational system may couple to a constrained subset of post-boundary paths. That does not establish direct contact with an underlying literal grid.

This distinction protects the model from turning a useful state-space representation into an unsupported physical object.

## 11. Cross-layer coupling

A valid coupling statement should specify:

```text
source address
→ coupling variable
→ receiving address
→ measurable effect
→ reduction rule
```

Example:

```text
I-layer coordination
→ shared timing and instruction
→ O/P-layer alignment
→ lower behavioural variance
→ no effect when coordination is scrambled
```

A coupling claim is weak when it merely notes resemblance.

It becomes stronger when changes at one address predict measured changes at another under controlled conditions.

---

## 12. Failure modes

### Fragmentation

One invariant is split into several named mechanisms because its layer expressions look different.

### Flattening

Different layers are treated as identical because they share a pattern.

### Metaphor leakage

Physical terms are used in cognitive or symbolic domains without a defined coupling.

### Observer inflation

Observer-state language substitutes for missing physical explanation.

### Substrate concretisation

A mathematical possibility space is turned into a literal material architecture without evidence.

### Address drift

A term begins with one definition and silently changes when moved between layers.

---

## 13. Falsifiers

The cross-layer invariant claim is weakened if:

- the supposed invariant cannot be stated independently of its examples;
- layer addresses require incompatible definitions;
- no lawful address map can be given;
- predicted cross-layer couplings fail repeatedly;
- one address survives only by borrowing evidence from another;
- simpler domain-specific explanations outperform the invariant model;
- the invariant generates no new discrimination, prediction, or compression.

A failed address does not automatically destroy the invariant at every layer. But repeated failure across addresses weakens the claim that the structure is genuinely cross-layer.

---

## 14. Compressed rule

> Preserve the invariant. Name the address. Change the variables honestly. Demand evidence at the layer where the claim is made.
